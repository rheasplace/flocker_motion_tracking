How this works

The concept: A body-tracking camera system designed for security and identification is repurposed as an instrument of erasure. The more you move, the more you're tracked, the more your image is destroyed. Each detection becomes a site of distortion.

1. Capture. The browser's getUserMedia grabs your webcam into an HTML <video> element, which p5.js draws to a canvas each frame.

2. Pose estimation. Every frame is passed to ml5.js's bodyPose, a wrapper around Google's MoveNet model (TensorFlow.js). MoveNet runs locally on your device — no video leaves your machine. It outputs 17 keypoint coordinates: the full skeleton.

3. Detection spawning. Each frame, with probability = detection rate, the system picks N random currently-visible keypoints and defines a region (polygon) between them. This is the "detection" moment — your body has been tracked.

4. Pixelation. Instead of drawing a shape, the system extracts the video pixels in that region and pixelates them — scrambling your image in the exact location the camera identified you. Higher scramble intensity = larger pixel blocks = more complete erasure.

5. Accumulation. Each new detection adds another layer of pixelation on top. As you move and generate more detections, your image progressively dissolves, replaced by the mosaic of countless "tracking events." The system meant to identify you becomes the means of your visual destruction.

Tuning: Low detection rate + high intensity = sparse, dramatic censorship marks. High rate + low intensity = dense, granular distortion covering your whole body. Region size (3 vs 5 points) changes the shape of the distortion zones — tight triangles vs loose, organic shapes.
